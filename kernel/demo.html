    The Grain of the Screen

    Notes on reading, rendering, and the persistence of text

    Every reading surface carries latent assumptions about how thought should move. The codex taught us that ideas progress — that a beginning precedes a middle and a middle precedes an end. The scroll implied something else: continuity, river-like, without the caesura of the page-turn. What does the LED screen imply?

    The first mass-market e-readers appeared in 2007. Within five years, long-form reading on backlit devices had become normalised — though the neurological literature on comprehension differences between ink and phosphor remains contested.

    It implies, above all, a kind of restlessness. The browser encodes impatience in its architecture: the tab bar, the back button, the address field. The text exists in competition with everything else the window could be showing. This is not a defect of readers but a feature of the container.

    Good typography, in this environment, is a form of argument. It argues: stay here. It makes the page feel safe enough, and quiet enough, and distinct enough from the surrounding noise that the eye is willing to commit. The designer's job is to minimise the activation energy required for sustained attention.

    On the persistence of the serif

    The serif question has been rehearsed so many times it has worn a groove in the literature. Sans or serif for screen? The research, when stripped of its confounds, returns a frustrating null: at modern display resolutions, it does not measurably matter. Gwern ran 142,983 visits across four typefaces — Baskerville, Georgia, Trebuchet, Helvetica — and found no statistically significant engagement difference. The most extreme pairwise result didn't breach p = 0.05.

    And yet the question persists because it is actually a question about something else. Serif type carries cultural information — it signals seriousness, duration, the expectation that the reader will stay. This is not legibility in the mechanical sense. It is legibility in the social sense: an agreement between writer and reader about what kind of reading is being invited.

    Matthew Butterick notes that Georgia and Calibri, both designed for low-DPI CRT screens, have "no special legibility benefit on today's screens." Their design compromises — open counters, exaggerated strokes — compensated for lost resolution that high-DPI displays no longer require.

    There are too many substantive matters to think about. I let it go at that — one serif and one sans-serif web font.

    Edward Tufte, on choosing the typeface for his website

    Tufte's indifference is instructive. He is not wrong that typography can become a distraction from content — the type obsessive who spends three days selecting a body face has perhaps misallocated the week. But the choice is not neutral. Indifference is itself a position.

    Contrast and the open iris

    Reading in the dark forces the iris open. A wider aperture means more light from bright areas bleeds into adjacent dark ones — the letterform halates, glows slightly at its edges, loses sharpness. This is why white text on pure black feels harsh to roughly half of readers, and why the "just use dark mode" response to eye strain is more complicated than it appears.

    Halation affects readers with astigmatism most acutely — roughly 47% of the population. The APCA standard (WCAG 3.0, in progress) corrects for the polarity-blindness of WCAG 2.x, which was derived from positive polarity only and systematically overstates contrast for light-on-dark pairings.

    The practitioner consensus — Google's Android dark theme ships with #121212 as background — solves the OLED smearing problem (pure black means pixels physically off; they lag when content scrolls over them) but addresses contrast mechanically, not perceptually. The result reads as correct by the numbers and corporate by the eye.

    The sweet spot is not a point but a basin — a range of warm near-blacks and muted off-whites within which readers can find a position that suits their eyes, ambient light, and physiology.

    The practical upshot: avoid pure values at either extreme. A background around #1c1914 — near-black with a warm cast — and body text around #d4c9b6 produces contrast that meets the APCA threshold without clinical glare. The warmth is not decorative. A brown-tinted dark reads as a lit room; a cold grey reads as a dead monitor.

    Scanning and flow

    The F-pattern — the eye-tracking signature of a reader who has decided not to fully read — is commonly described as an inevitable property of human attention on screens. The more useful reframe is that it is a failure state, not a given. It arises when the text provides no rhythm for the eye to follow. The cure is not fragmentation — it is structure.

    There is a meaningful difference between two strategies for serving both scanners and readers simultaneously:

    • Chunking — breaking prose into visually separated units with heavy subheading use. Serves scanners. Destroys flow. The argument that depends on sequential reading cannot survive it.
    • Semantic zoom — adding a scan layer (headings, margin notes) on top of uninterrupted prose. Scanners navigate via the layer. Readers ignore it. Neither mode is compromised.

    The second strategy is Gwern's term, and it is the more defensible one for documents that have a thesis. Margin notes play a structural role beyond annotation — they function as a compressed index of the main argument, visible to the scanner without interrupting the reading line.

    This extends the Tufte sidenote system, which uses margin notes primarily for captions and figure annotations. The semantic zoom argument claims they can also carry the scan layer of an argumentative document without requiring subheadings every two paragraphs.

    Measure

    Forty-five to ninety characters per line: the range cited most often. Sixty-five to sixty-eight: the range cited most reliably. Eric Lawler validated 66 characters against 66 physical books. Firefox Reader mode auto-constrains to approximately 68. The convergence reflects the distance the eye can comfortably travel before returning to find the start of the next line without losing its place.

    On a display, measure is the most neglected variable. Most websites run full-width text to the viewport edge, producing lines of 120 characters or more. The experience is one of mild continuous effort — reading, but working slightly harder than necessary on every line. The cumulative cost, across a long document, is fatigue that the reader attributes to the content rather than the container.

    /* The constraint that matters most */
p {
  max-width:   65ch;   /* ~65 characters */
  line-height: 1.5;    /* ~150% of base size */
  font-size:   17px;   /* minimum comfortable size */
}

    The fix is one line of CSS. Its persistence as an unsolved problem is a design philosophy failure, not a technical one — the assumption that more width means more value, the reluctance to surrender real estate to empty air.

    These are starting notes, not conclusions. The goal is a set of primitives stable enough to build on — not a finished aesthetic, but a baseline from which variation is legible as variation, and deviation is recognisable as a choice.
